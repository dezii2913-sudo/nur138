1) Terminology (Definition • Medical Sentence • Example • Nursing • Exam Trap)

Tap/scroll as needed. This section is designed to make test questions readable.

Pharmacology

Definition The study of drugs and their effects on the body.
Medical sentence Pharmacology explains why the same medication can help one patient and harm another.
Example Kidney disease can cause drug buildup, increasing toxicity risk.
Nursing Use pharmacology to predict what to assess, what to monitor, and what teaching matters most.
Exam trap “Pharm” questions often test thinking (safety/monitoring), not memorizing.

Pharmacokinetics (ADME)

Definition What the body does to the drug: absorption, distribution, metabolism, excretion.
Medical sentence Renal impairment slows excretion and raises drug levels.
Example A normal dose becomes toxic when kidneys can’t clear it.
Nursing Think ADME anytime liver/kidneys are compromised or doses change.
Exam trap PK is movement/processing of the drug—not the desired effect.

Pharmacodynamics

Definition What the drug does to the body (mechanism of action + response).
Medical sentence Beta blockers lower heart rate by blocking beta‑1 receptors.
Example HR drops after metoprolol—this is the drug’s effect on the body.
Nursing Use pharmacodynamics to predict side effects and what to monitor.
Exam trap PD ≠ ADME. PD = effect; PK = processing.

Pharmacotherapeutics

Definition Using medications to treat, prevent, or manage disease and improve patient outcomes.
Medical sentence Pharmacotherapeutics connects the drug choice to the patient’s condition and goals.
Example Choosing an analgesic plan that controls pain while protecting breathing.
Nursing Ask: “What is the drug for? What outcome should improve? What will I reassess?”
Exam trap If you can state the reason + expected outcome, you can eliminate wrong answers fast.

Side effect

Definition A predictable, often unavoidable secondary effect of a medication.
Medical sentence Drowsiness is a common side effect of many antihistamines.
Example A patient takes diphenhydramine and feels sleepy.
Nursing Teach what to expect and when to call the provider.
Exam trap Side effect ≠ allergy. Nausea/drowsiness is usually not a true allergy.

Adverse drug reaction (ADR)

Definition An unintended, harmful reaction that occurs at normal doses.
Medical sentence A severe rash and swelling after a first dose may indicate an ADR.
Example Hives and wheezing after an antibiotic.
Nursing Stop the med (if indicated), assess airway/breathing, and notify provider.
Exam trap ADRs can be dose-related or allergic—look at symptoms.

Narrow therapeutic index (NTI)

Definition Small margin between therapeutic and toxic levels—requires close monitoring.
Medical sentence Drugs with NTI often require lab levels and dose precision.
Example Small dose changes can cause toxicity if the safe range is narrow.
Nursing Verify dosing, check labs, and monitor for toxicity signs.
Exam trap “Monitor levels closely” is a clue for NTI concepts.

Steady state

Definition Point where drug intake equals drug elimination (blood levels plateau).
Medical sentence Many drugs reach steady state after about 4–5 half‑lives.
Example A medication takes several days to reach full effect because steady state isn’t reached yet.
Nursing Don’t expect full therapeutic effect immediately for long half‑life drugs.
Exam trap If a med “takes time,” think half‑life → steady state.

Loading dose

Definition A higher initial dose given to quickly achieve therapeutic levels.
Medical sentence Loading doses are used when waiting for steady state would take too long.
Example A patient receives an initial larger dose, then smaller maintenance doses.
Nursing Monitor closely—loading doses can increase adverse effects.
Exam trap Loading dose ≠ “double the dose.” It’s calculated and purposeful.

Medication reconciliation

Definition Comparing a patient’s current/home meds with new orders to prevent errors.
Medical sentence Medication reconciliation is performed at admission, transfer, and discharge.
Example Catching duplicate acetaminophen in two different OTC products.
Nursing Ask about OTC, herbals, and “as needed” meds—patients forget these.
Exam trap Many med errors happen during transitions of care.

Antagonist

Definition A drug that blocks or reverses another drug’s effect by competing at the same receptor.
Medical sentence Naloxone is an opioid antagonist used in suspected opioid overdose.
Example Morphine slows breathing; naloxone is given and breathing improves.
Nursing Know reversal/blocking drugs and when to give them; monitor response and recurrence.
Exam trap Antagonist ≠ universal antidote. It only works for specific receptor-drug pairs.

Antagonistic effects

Definition When two drugs oppose each other, reducing one or both effects.
Medical sentence Beta blockers may antagonize beta-agonist bronchodilators.
Example A patient on metoprolol feels their albuterol works less well.
Nursing Watch for decreased therapeutic effect and notify provider if control worsens.
Exam trap Not the same as side effects—this is drug vs drug opposition.

Bioavailability

Definition How much of a drug reaches the bloodstream unchanged.
Medical sentence Oral drugs may have lower bioavailability due to first-pass metabolism.
Example A smaller IV dose can work like a larger oral dose.
Nursing If route changes (IV→PO), reassess effect; anticipate dose differences.
Exam trap Bioavailability isn’t absorption only—metabolism affects it too.

Blood–brain barrier

Definition A protective barrier that limits many drugs from entering the brain.
Medical sentence Lipid‑soluble drugs cross the blood–brain barrier more easily.
Example Some antibiotics don’t treat meningitis well unless they cross the BBB.
Nursing Monitor pediatrics/older adults for CNS effects (their BBB can be more permeable).
Exam trap BBB affects CNS access, not kidney excretion.

Contraindication

Definition A specific reason a drug should NOT be used for a patient.
Medical sentence Atropine is contraindicated in angle‑closure glaucoma.
Example Atropine can raise eye pressure and worsen glaucoma.
Nursing Check history first; hold and notify provider if contraindicated.
Exam trap Contraindication ≠ precaution. Contraindication = do not give.

Drug actions (mechanism of action)

Definition How a drug produces an effect in the body.
Medical sentence Metoprolol lowers heart rate by blocking beta‑1 receptors.
Example Blocking beta‑1 slows the heart.
Nursing Use mechanism to predict effects, side effects, and what to monitor.
Exam trap Knowing the ‘why’ helps eliminate wrong answers.

Drug classification

Definition Grouping drugs by what they do or how they work.
Medical sentence Metoprolol is a beta blocker used for hypertension.
Example Most beta blockers slow heart rate and lower BP.
Nursing If you know the class, you can predict class side effects/contraindications.
Exam trap Class effects apply across many drugs—don’t memorize one by one only.

Drug interaction

Definition When a drug’s effect is changed by another drug, food, or supplement.
Medical sentence St. John’s wort can reduce effectiveness of many medications.
Example A patient’s antidepressant works less after starting St. John’s wort.
Nursing Ask about OTC/herbals; monitor for reduced effect or toxicity.
Exam trap ‘Natural’ products can still interact strongly.

First-pass effect

Definition Liver metabolism that happens before an oral drug reaches circulation.
Medical sentence Drugs with high first‑pass metabolism need higher oral doses.
Example Oral nitroglycerin is ineffective; it’s given sublingually instead.
Nursing Explains why route matters and why some drugs aren’t given orally.
Exam trap Does not apply to IV dosing.

Half-life

Definition Time it takes for 50% of a drug to be eliminated from the body.
Medical sentence Long half‑life drugs can accumulate if dosing is too frequent.
Example A long half‑life med builds up over days, increasing toxicity risk.
Nursing Guides dosing interval and when to watch for accumulation/toxicity.
Exam trap Half‑life ≠ onset. It’s about elimination, not start time.

Intraarterial

Definition Injection into an artery.
Medical sentence The medication was administered intraarterially during a procedure.
Example Contrast dye may be delivered via an arterial line during imaging.
Nursing High‑risk route; verify order/route carefully and monitor site/ischemia.
Exam trap Don’t confuse with IV (vein) administration.

Intraarticular

Definition Injection into a joint space.
Medical sentence The provider ordered an intraarticular corticosteroid injection.
Example Steroid injected into a knee to reduce inflammation.
Nursing Assess pain/infection signs; educate about post‑procedure care.
Exam trap Intraarticular ≠ intramuscular.

Intrathecal

Definition Injection into the spinal canal (CSF).
Medical sentence Chemotherapy may be administered intrathecally.
Example Medication placed into CSF to reach brain/spinal cord.
Nursing Very high‑risk; double‑check drug/dose/route; monitor neuro changes.
Exam trap Wrong route can be fatal—route is critical.

Onset of action

Definition How long it takes for a drug to start working.
Medical sentence IV medications usually have a rapid onset of action.
Example IV pain meds reduce pain faster than oral meds.
Nursing Use onset to time reassessment and safety monitoring.
Exam trap Onset ≠ peak; onset is first effect, peak is strongest.

Peak level

Definition The highest concentration of a drug in the blood.
Medical sentence Peak levels may be monitored to avoid toxicity.
Example Too‑high peak can increase adverse effects/toxicity.
Nursing Time labs correctly; assess for toxicity at peak times.
Exam trap Peak ≠ trough; peak is highest, trough is lowest.

Synergistic effects

Definition Two drugs together cause a stronger effect than expected.
Medical sentence Opioids and benzodiazepines can synergistically depress breathing.
Example Morphine + lorazepam → excessive sedation/low RR.
Nursing Prioritize respiratory assessment; hold meds and notify provider as needed.
Exam trap Synergistic ≠ additive; synergy is bigger-than-expected.

Therapeutic effects

Definition The intended, beneficial effect of a medication.
Medical sentence The therapeutic effect of albuterol is improved breathing.
Example Wheezing decreases after albuterol use.
Nursing Evaluate whether the goal was met and document the response.
Exam trap Side effects can occur even when therapeutic effect is present.

Tolerance

Definition Decreased response over time, requiring higher doses for the same effect.
Medical sentence Opioid tolerance may require dose adjustment over time.
Example A patient needs a higher opioid dose to achieve the same pain relief.
Nursing Differentiate tolerance from addiction; monitor for safety.
Exam trap Tolerance ≠ allergy or toxicity.

Toxic

Definition A drug level or effect that is harmful/dangerous.
Medical sentence Signs of lithium toxicity require immediate action.
Example Confusion and tremors suggest toxic levels.
Nursing Recognize early toxicity and intervene; check levels as ordered.
Exam trap Toxicity can happen from accumulation or interactions.

Trade name

Definition The brand/manufacturer name of a drug.
Medical sentence Tylenol is a trade name for acetaminophen.
Example Two cold medicines may both contain acetaminophen under different brands.
Nursing Prevent double dosing by checking ingredients and generic names.
Exam trap Exams usually use generic names more often.

Trough level

Definition The lowest concentration of a drug in the blood, drawn before the next dose.
Medical sentence A vancomycin trough is drawn just before the next dose.
Example Low trough → drug may be ineffective; high trough → toxicity risk.
Nursing Time lab draws correctly and coordinate with dosing schedule.
Exam trap Trough is NOT drawn at peak effect.
2) Foundations
Pharmacokinetics (ADME) — what the body does to the drug
  • Absorption: drug enters the bloodstream (route matters).
  • Distribution: drug travels to tissues (protein binding, fat/water balance).
  • Metabolism: drug is broken down (mostly liver).
  • Excretion: drug leaves the body (mostly kidneys).
Nursing If liver/kidneys are impaired, drugs can build up → toxicity. Reassess often after dose changes.

Nursing Process & Medications (ADPIE)
  • Assessment: allergies (true allergy vs intolerance), complete med list (Rx/OTC/herbal), last dose times, vitals, pain score, weight (kg), pregnancy/breastfeeding, organ function (renal/hepatic), ability to swallow, IV/site assessment.
  • Diagnosis: medication-related nursing diagnoses (examples): Risk for injury, Risk for adverse drug reaction, Deficient knowledge, Ineffective health management, Acute pain.
  • Planning: set measurable goals tied to therapeutic effects + safety plan (hold parameters, lab timing, follow-up assessments, teaching plan).
  • Implementation: apply all medication rights, prepare/administer correctly, patient teaching, use safety tech (bar-code scanning/smart pumps), document immediately.
  • Evaluation: reassess for therapeutic response, side effects/ADRs, toxicity, patient understanding; decide if med should be continued, adjusted, or held.
Exam focus ADPIE always includes D = Diagnosis. If it’s missing, that’s the trick.
3) Safety + Lifespan + OTC/Herbal
Medication Error Prevention (what instructors love)

Expanded Medication Rights

  • Right patient (2 identifiers)
  • Right medication (generic vs trade; look-alike/sound-alike)
  • Right dose (weight-based, maximums, calculations)
  • Right route (never assume; verify formulation)
  • Right time (critical times; relation to meals; trough timing)
  • Right documentation (immediately after giving)
  • Right reason (indication matches patient condition)
  • Right response (reassess for effect + adverse effects)
  • Right education (purpose, side effects, when to call, how to take)
  • Right assessment (vitals, labs, allergies, contraindications before giving)
  • Right to refuse (respect + educate + document)

Medication Error Types (Unit 1 must-know)

  • Prescribing: wrong drug/dose/route, illegible/incomplete order
  • Transcription: wrong entry into MAR/EHR
  • Dispensing: pharmacy supplies wrong drug/dose/label
  • Administration (most tested): wrong patient/dose/route/time, omitted dose
  • Monitoring: failure to assess vitals/labs/levels or recognize toxicity
  • Documentation: incorrect or missing documentation
Exam trap “What should the nurse do first?” is usually assessment/monitoring or hold + clarify—not “just give it.”
Safety essentials Use two identifiers. Use leading zero (0.5 mg) and no trailing zero (1 mg, not 1.0 mg). Clarify unclear orders—never guess. High-risk: pediatrics, pregnancy, older adults, renal/hepatic disease, polypharmacy.
OTC & Herbal
  • OTC meds can interact and can mask symptoms, delaying diagnosis.
  • Herbals can vary in strength/purity and may not be regulated like Rx drugs.
  • Teach: “Natural” does not mean safe.

Black Box Warning (Boxed Warning)

Definition: The FDA’s strongest warning for a prescription medication—signals a risk of serious or life‑threatening adverse effects.
  • Nursing priority: verify the warning, assess risk factors, teach what to report immediately, and monitor closely.
  • Test angle: If a question mentions “boxed warning,” it’s usually a priority/safety question.

Legal Considerations in Drug Therapy (Nursing Practice)

Core legal themes: safety, documentation, scope, and accountability.
  • Scope of practice: administer meds only with a valid order and within facility policy; question unsafe orders.
  • Informed consent & education: ensure the patient understands purpose/risks; reinforce provider teaching; document education.
  • Documentation is legal protection: chart medication, time, route, response, and any refusal or adverse effect.
  • Negligence/malpractice risk: failing standards (wrong dose/route, failure to monitor, failure to respond).
  • Controlled substances: follow chain-of-custody, wasting rules, counts, and secure storage.
  • Error reporting: assess patient first, notify provider/charge nurse, follow reporting policy; chart facts (no blame).
4) Analgesics (Pain Basics)
Opioids vs Non-opioids
  • Opioids: strongest pain relief, highest risk for respiratory depression.
  • NSAIDs: pain/inflammation, risks GI bleed/kidney injury.
  • Acetaminophen: pain/fever, risk liver injury at high doses.
Exam trap Opioids + benzodiazepines = synergy → respiratory depression risk.

Nicotinic vs Muscarinic receptors (what they DO)
Nicotinic
Where: neuromuscular junction (skeletal muscle), autonomic ganglia, adrenal medulla
Does: turns signals ON (neuromuscular contraction / autonomic relay)
Exam clue: higher-dose ACh effects can “spill” into nicotinic → twitching/weakness
Muscarinic
Where: organs (heart, lungs, GI, bladder, eyes, glands)
Does: “rest & digest” actions (slow heart, tighten bronchi, move GI, urinate)
Exam clue: most nursing adverse effects are muscarinic (bradycardia, bronchospasm, diarrhea)
EXAM FOCUS (Instructor highlights)
  • Nicotinic receptors
  • Muscarinic receptors
  • Named nicotinic because they can be stimulated by the alkaloid nicotine
  • Key roles include neuromuscular contraction, autonomic regulation, cognitive functions like memory and attention, and they are implicated in nicotine addiction and Alzheimer’s disease.
  • Nicotinic Receptors
  • Named muscarinic because they can be stimulated by the alkaloid muscarine
  • involved in peristalsis, micturition, bronchoconstriction, and several other parasympathetic reactions

Cholinergic Drugs (Parasympathomimetics)

These drugs increase acetylcholine (ACh) or act like ACh → your patient looks more “PNS‑dominant.”

Direct‑acting vs Indirect‑acting (must know)
Direct‑acting cholinergics
MOA: Bind directly to ACh receptors (usually muscarinic)
Examples (generic → trade):
Bethanechol → Urecholine (urinary retention)
Pilocarpine → Salagen / Isopto Carpine (glaucoma, dry mouth)
Mnemonic
“Beth brings back the bladder.”
Indirect‑acting cholinergics
MOA: Inhibit acetylcholinesterase → ACh builds up
Examples (generic → trade):
Pyridostigmine → Mestinon (MG maintenance)
Donepezil → Aricept (Alzheimer’s)
Exam clue
Indirect drugs can cause cholinergic crisis if overdosed.
Cholinergic effects, adverse effects, contraindications
Drug effects
  • ↓HR / ↓AV conduction
  • Bronchoconstriction + ↑secretions
  • ↑GI motility + ↑secretions
  • Urination (↑bladder contraction)
  • Miosis (pupil constriction)
Adverse effects (“too much ACh”)
DUMBBELSS: Diarrhea, Urination, Miosis, Bradycardia, Bronchospasm, Emesis, Lacrimation, Sweating, Salivation.
Translation: “wet + slow + tight lungs.”
Contraindications / When NOT to use (high yield)
  • Asthma/COPD
  • Bradycardia / hypotension
  • GI obstruction
  • Urinary obstruction
Cholinergic crisis vs Myasthenic crisis
Cholinergic crisis: too much ACh → DUMBBELSS + weakness. Antidote often includes atropine (as ordered).
Myasthenic crisis: not enough ACh at NMJ → worsening weakness (often needs airway support).

Anticholinergic Drugs (Parasympatholytics)

These drugs block muscarinic receptors → patient looks more “SNS‑dominant.” Think: DRY + FAST.

Core effects, adverse effects, contraindications
MOA + Effects
  • ↑HR
  • Bronchodilation + ↓secretions
  • ↓GI motility (constipation)
  • Urine retention
  • Mydriasis (pupil dilation)
  • ↓Sweating (heat risk)
Mnemonic
“Hot, Dry, Blind, Fast, Constipated.”
Examples (generic → trade)
Scopolamine → (motion sickness)
Ipratropium → Atrovent
Diphenhydramine → Benadryl
Nursing focus
Monitor urinary retention, confusion (elderly), overheating.
Contraindications / When NOT to use (high yield)
  • Narrow‑angle glaucoma
  • BPH / urinary retention risk
  • Elderly / dementia risk
  • Heat exposure / fever

Answer Key + Rationales (Why others are wrong)

Use this section after you attempt the questions above.

SNS Key
  1. Q1: B — Propranolol blocks β2 → bronchoconstriction/wheezing. A is heart. C is vessels. D is PNS.
  2. Q2: C — Stop infusion + prepare phentolamine (Regitine). A worsens. B alone is insufficient. D unrelated.
  3. Q3: C — α1 = vasoconstriction → ↑BP. A is PNS. B is α1 blocker effect. D is cholinergic.
  4. Q4: A — Alpha blockers → first-dose hypotension. Others not classic.
  5. Q5: B — Beta blockers mask hypoglycemia signs (tremor/tachycardia). C/D unrelated.
  6. Q6: C — Nonselective β blocker = highest asthma risk. A/B/D more β1-selective.
  7. Q7: A — First dose bedtime reduces fall risk (orthostatic). B/C/D unsafe.
  8. Q8: A — Abrupt stop → rebound HTN/angina. B/C/D are PNS.
EXAM FOCUS (Instructor highlights)
  • Effect on receptors on prostate gland and bladder decreases resistance to urinary outflow, thus reducing urinary obstruction and relieving effects of benign prostatic hyperplasia (BPH).
  • Phentolamine
  • Quickly reverses the potent vasoconstrictive effects of extravasated vasopressors such as norepinephrine or epinephrine
  • When phentolamine is used to diagnose the presence of pheochromocytoma, the nurse will assess for what indicative finding?
  • To help establish a diagnosis of pheochromocytoma, a single intravenous dose of phentolamine is given to the hypertensive patient who is suspected of having the tumor. If the blood pressure declines rapidly, it is highly likely that the patient has a pheochromocytoma.
  • The primary adverse effects of alpha blockers are those related to their effects on the vasculature. First-dose phenomenon, which is a severe and sudden drop in blood pressure after the administration of the first dose of an alpha-adrenergic blocker, can cause patients to fall or pass out. All patients must be warned about this adverse effect before they take their first dose of an alpha blocker. Orthostatic hypotension can occur with any dose of an alpha blocker, and patients must be warned to get up slowly from a supine position.

5) ANS (SNS vs PNS)
Quick system map

SNS (fight/flight): ↑HR, ↑BP, bronchodilation, ↓digestion

PNS (rest/digest): ↓HR, ↑digestion, urination, secretions

Nursing If a symptom is “wet” (salivation/diarrhea), think cholinergic. If “dry/hot/confused,” think anticholinergic.
6) Lifespan Pharmacology (ADME by Age Group)

Unit 1 high-yield: how age changes absorption, distribution, metabolism, and excretion—and what that means for safety.

Why Lifespan Pharmacology Matters (Exam GOLD)

Medication absorption, distribution, metabolism, and excretion (ADME) change significantly across the lifespan. These changes directly affect dosing, drug response, and toxicity risk.

NCLEX Reality: Many "priority" questions are really lifespan questions in disguise.

Neonates & Infants

ADME ComponentKey ChangesNursing Implication
AbsorptionHigher gastric pH, delayed gastric emptyingOral meds absorb slower/unpredictably
DistributionHigh body water, low protein bindingHigher free drug levels → toxicity risk
MetabolismImmature liver enzymesProlonged half-life, accumulation
ExcretionImmature kidneysSlower clearance
High-Risk Meds: Opioids, benzodiazepines, antibiotics with narrow therapeutic windows

Children (Pediatrics)

FocusKey Points
DosingAlways weight-based (kg)
MetabolismOften faster than adults → may need higher mg/kg doses
SafetyLiquid concentrations vary – double-check calculations
Problematic Meds: Aspirin (Reye syndrome), tetracyclines, fluoroquinolones

Pregnant Patients

ConsiderationImplication
Placental transferMany drugs cross placenta
Volume expansionLower serum drug concentrations
MetabolismAltered hepatic enzyme activity
High-Risk Categories: Teratogens, NSAIDs late pregnancy, isotretinoin, ACE inhibitors

Older Adults (Geriatrics)

ADMEAge-Related ChangeClinical Effect
AbsorptionSlightly delayedUsually minimal impact
DistributionIncreased body fat, decreased albuminDrug accumulation (fat-soluble meds)
MetabolismReduced liver blood flowLonger half-life
Excretion↓ GFRMajor ↑ toxicity risk
Problematic Meds: Benzodiazepines, anticholinergics, opioids, NSAIDs

BIG Lifespan Takeaways (Exam Traps)

  • Renal function matters more than age alone
  • Start low, go slow (geriatrics)
  • Weight-based dosing is non-negotiable in pediatrics
  • Fat-soluble drugs linger longer in older adults
  • Immature organs = accumulation risk in neonates
Clinical Judgment: Many adverse drug reactions are predictable based on age-related ADME changes.
7) Drug Interactions: Cancel-Outs vs Enhancers

Unit 1 foundation: synergistic, additive, and antagonistic effects + common OTC/herbal risks.

Types of Drug Interactions (EXAM FOUNDATION)

  • Synergistic: Combined effect is greater than expected
  • Additive: Combined effect equals the sum of each drug
  • Antagonistic: One drug reduces or blocks the effect of another
NCLEX Tip: Synergistic interactions are usually the most dangerous.

Common Synergistic (ENHANCING) Drug Combinations

Drug CombinationEffectNursing Concern
Opioids + BenzodiazepinesRespiratory depressionMonitor RR, O₂ sat, LOC
Opioids + AlcoholExcessive CNS depressionHigh overdose risk
ACE inhibitors + DiureticsEnhanced BP loweringHypotension risk
Anticoagulants + NSAIDs↑ Bleeding riskMonitor for bleeding
Anticholinergics + AntihistaminesDry mouth, retention, confusionAnticholinergic burden

Common Antagonistic (CANCELING) Drug Combinations

Drug PairWhat HappensExam Focus
Beta blockers + Beta agonistsReduced bronchodilationAsthma/COPD risk
Naloxone + OpioidsOpioid reversalKnow antidotes
NSAIDs + AntihypertensivesDecreased BP controlBP elevation
Antacids + Oral medsReduced absorptionTiming matters
Exam Trap: Antagonism does NOT mean side effects—it means reduced effectiveness.

Additive Effects (Often Confused with Synergy)

ExampleResult
Two antihypertensivesPredictable BP lowering

OTC & Herbal Interactions (VERY HIGH YIELD)

ProductInteracts WithResult

BIG Unit 1 Interaction Takeaways

  • Polypharmacy increases interaction risk exponentially
  • Older adults are most vulnerable
  • Always ask about OTC and herbal use
  • Synergistic CNS depressants are life-threatening
  • Antagonism often explains “med not working” complaints
7) Quick-Scan: Interactions & Red Flags (Unit 1)

High-risk “Enhancers” (Synergy) — think: bigger-than-expected effect

Priority danger CNS depressants together (opioids + benzos + alcohol) → respiratory depression. Monitor RR, O₂ sat, LOC; hold/notify per protocol.
  • Opioids + benzodiazepines → respiratory depression
  • Opioids + alcohol → severe sedation/overdose risk
  • Anticoagulants + NSAIDs → bleeding risk
  • Anticholinergics + antihistamines → confusion/urinary retention (especially older adults)

Common “Cancel-Outs” (Antagonism)

Exam trap Antagonism = reduced therapeutic effect (not “side effects”). Often shows up as “the med isn’t working.”
  • Beta blockers + beta agonists (e.g., metoprolol + albuterol) → reduced bronchodilation
  • Naloxone + opioids → opioid reversal
  • Antacids + some oral meds → reduced absorption (separate dosing when instructed)
  • NSAIDs + some antihypertensives → less BP control

OTC/Herbal “Gotchas”

  • St. John’s wort → can reduce effectiveness of several meds (ask every time)
  • Ginkgo + anticoagulants → bleeding risk
  • Ginseng + antidiabetics → hypoglycemia risk
Nursing Always ask: “What vitamins, supplements, teas, or herbs do you take?”

8) NCLEX-Style Self-Test: Lifespan + ADME + Interactions (Unit 1)

Tap a question to reveal the answer + why. These are designed from Unit 1 foundations only.

Q1. An older adult is prescribed a new medication. Which age-related change most increases the risk for drug toxicity?
Answer Decreased renal function (↓ GFR) leading to slower excretion and drug accumulation.
Why others are wrong Absorption changes are usually minor; distribution changes matter but renal clearance is the biggest driver of toxicity risk.
Q2. A pediatric dose is ordered. What is the nurse’s priority before administration?
Answer Verify weight in kilograms and calculate the dose using weight-based dosing.
Exam trap Pounds must be converted to kg; do not “estimate.”
Q3. Which route bypasses first-pass metabolism and is often used for rapid onset without IV access?
Answer Sublingual (also inhalation and transdermal bypass first pass; rectal bypasses partially).
Why oral is wrong Oral medications commonly undergo first-pass metabolism.
Q4. A patient takes metoprolol and reports their rescue inhaler “doesn’t work as well.” What is the best explanation?
Answer Antagonistic interaction: beta blockers can reduce beta-agonist bronchodilation.
Nursing Assess breathing, notify provider if control is worsening.
Q5. A client is receiving an opioid and a benzodiazepine. What assessment is priority?
Answer Respiratory status (RR, depth, O₂ sat, level of sedation).
Why This is a synergistic CNS depressant combination → respiratory depression risk.
Q6. A neonate receives a medication. Why are neonates at higher risk for drug accumulation?
Answer Immature liver enzymes and immature renal function → slower metabolism and excretion.
Q7. The client says, “I’m taking St. John’s wort so I stopped my prescription.” What is the nurse’s best response?
Answer “Let’s review the risks and evidence together, including possible interactions, and involve your provider.”
Nursing Herbs can interact and vary in strength/purity.
